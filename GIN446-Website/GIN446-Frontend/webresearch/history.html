History of the World Wide Web

What is the World Wide Web?

The World Wide Web is a global information medium that users can access via computers connected to the Internet. The Web gives users access to a vast array of mass media and content—via the deep web, the dark web, and the commonly accessible surface web—that is connected by means of hypertext or hypermedia links.

The Web operates within the Internet's basic client-server format; servers are computer programs that store and transmit documents to other computers on the network when asked to, while clients are programs that request documents from a server as the user asks for them.

How was it invented?

The development of the World Wide Web was begun in 1989 by Tim Berners-Lee and his colleagues at CERN, an international scientific organization based in Geneva, Switzerland. The WWW began as a project called ENQUIRE, which relied on a protocol, HyperText Transfer Protocol (HTTP), which standardized communication between servers and clients.

On August 6, 1991, the world's first website—info.cern.ch—went live at CERN, running on Tim Berners-Lee's NeXT computer.

How did it spread worldwide?

In 1991, Berners-Lee released his WWW software. Later on in the year, he announced the WWW software on Internet newsgroups and interest in the project spread around the world.

Thanks to the efforts of Paul Kunz and Louise Addis, the first Web server in the US came online in December 1991, once again in a particle physics laboratory: The SLAC in California.

Early in 1993, the National Center for Supercomputing Applications (NCSA) at the University of Illinois released a first version of its Mosaic browser that ran in the X Window System environment, and shortly afterwards released versions for the PC and Macintosh environments.

On 30 April 1993, CERN made the source code of WorldWideWeb available on a royalty-free basis, making it free software. By late 1993 there were over 500 known web servers, and the WWW accounted for 1% of internet traffic, which seemed a lot in those days.
By the end of 1994, the Web had 10 000 servers - 2000 of which were commercial - and 10 million users. Traffic was equivalent to shipping the entire collected works of Shakespeare every second.

Current trends

Rise of artificial intelligence

Web design is incorporating greater personalization and artificial intelligence, with features like chatbots providing customized user experiences, predictive analytics enhancing customer journeys, and natural language processing improving interaction.

By utilizing artificial intelligence, businesses can offer each visitor a uniquely tailored conversation that not only streamlines navigation but also boosts conversion rates as it drives more relevant content towards potential customers.

Serverless Architecture

Serverless architecture is an approach to software design that allows developers to build and run services without having to manage the underlying infrastructure. Developers can write and deploy code, while a cloud provider provisions servers to run their applications, databases, and storage systems at any scale.

Serverless computing offers an efficient way to build and manage web applications by reducing system overload, data loss, and server costs.

Dark Mode Themes

Dark Mode used to just be a trendy feature that served little purpose, but it has evolved from that point into an essential aspect of web design. It offers visual comfort, saves battery life, and provides a sleek, modern aesthetic.

Implementing dark mode goes beyond flipping the color palette; it requires careful consideration of color schemes, contrast levels, and readability to ensure a seamless transition between themes.

Inclusive Design

Inclusive design is a critical aspect of web development that makes sure that everyone can access your site and experience everything you have to offer. That includes people living with disabilities, folks on older devices, and people surfing the web while their computer is on the fritz.It makes the experience better for all of us.
Its practices not only comply with some legal requirements but also extend the reach of the website to a wider audience.

Blockchain Technology

A blockchain is a distributed database or ledger shared across a computer network's nodes. lockchains can be used to make data in any industry immutable—meaning it cannot be altered.

Blockchain follows an innovative approach to recording, verifying, and storing data. As it is based on decentralized and transparent mechanisms of data management, web development companies are embracing this outstanding technology for building future-ready websites and applications.

This report from Statista shows how blockchain technology is used in different organizations these days.

These organizations are integrating blockchain into their websites, applications, and multiple digital systems to create a secure environment for their customers.

Future possibilities

Virtual Reality (VR) and Augmented Reality (AR)

By seemlessly blending digital elements with the real world (AR) or immersing users in virtual realms (VR), web designers can create environments that transcend the conventional, providing users with a heightened sense of interaction and connectivity.

AR facilitates interactive experiences by overlaying digital content onto the user’s physical surroundings, encouraging exploration and real-time interaction.

On the other hand, VR immerses users in a simulated environment, where their actions dictate the narrative, resulting in a more profound and engaging interaction.

Conversational User Interface

With the rise of smart speakers and voice-activated devices, voice-based user interfaces are becoming essential to web design. They facilitate a natural human conversation between humans and machines

Designing for voice requires focusing on natural language processing and making content easily navigable through voice commands. Conversation UIs are much more achievable with machine learning and AI integration.

One path in which chatbots may evolve is via hybrid interfaces, which uses a combination of NLP-based voice/text chatbot systems along with a GUI, in order to give customers a familiar, yet natural experience.
Optimizing websites for voice search is no longer an option, but a necessity for accessibility and optimal user experience.

No-Code/Low-Code Development Experience

No-Code/Low-Code development experience allows users without expertise in programming to create web applications using a visual interface and drag-and-drop tools.
The necessity for organizations to quickly and efficiently build unique web apps without relying on expensive and hard-to-find developers has given rise to such web development trends.

According to Straits Research, by 2030, the market for low-code is expected to reach $148.5 billion. Low-code or no-code products provide ease and a low learning curve for anyone who wants to build a tech product, even helping developers build the initial phase and scale it up.

Motion User Interface

Motion UI is the art of bringing a web UI design to life through well-thought animations and transitions. The effectiveness of Motion UI doesn’t just rely on its animation; it rests on applying a few important principles that ensure that these animations are visually appealing and serve a purpose.

Even in the field of Augmented Reality, there are multiple options for motion design. So far, mainly advertising banners and inserted videos have moved there. The entire page may move in the future.

Its use is now gaining more popularity among websites. It has created a good user experience and has expanded on the features of websites and applications. Within the coming years, it is expected that 99.9 percent of websites will use Motion UI in their designs and templates.

Progressive Web Apps (PWAs)

Progressive Web Apps (PWAs) have been around for a few years, but they are likely to gain even more popularity in the upcoming years. PWAs are web applications that offer a native app-like experience, with features like push notifications, offline access, and smooth navigation. They are cheaper and easier to develop than native apps, making them a more cost-effective option for businesses.

Emerging trends and technologies such as WebAssembly, Web Components, and advanced APIs are driving the evolution of Progressive Web App development. These innovations hold the key to unlocking new capabilities, enhancing performance and expanding the reach of PWAs in the years to come.

Browser history

Emergence of Web Browsers

In 1990, Tim Berners-Lee created the first graphical web browser while work at CERN. A year later, Berners-Lee asked CERN math student Nicola Pellow to write the Line Mode Browser, a program for basic computer terminals.

In 1993, the web exploded. Universities, governments, and private corporations all saw opportunity in the open internet. Everyone needed new computer programs to access it. That year, Mosaic was created and became the very first popular web browser and the early ancestor of Mozilla Firefox.

In 1994, Marc Andreesseen (creator of Mosaic) founded Netscape and released Netscape Navigator to the public. It was wildly successful, and the first browser for the people. It was also the first move in a new kind of war for internet users.

The Browser Wars

By 1995, Netscape Navigator wasn’t the only way to get online. Computer software giant Microsoft licensed the old Mosaic code and built its own window to the web, Internet Explorer. The release sparked a war. Netscape and Microsoft worked feverishly to make new versions of their programs, each attempting to outdo the other with faster, better products.

Netscape created and released JavaScript, which gave websites powerful computing capabilities they never had before. Microsoft countered with Cascading Style Sheets (CSS), which became the standard for web page design.

In 1996, the Norwegian company Opera Software launched the Opera web browser, introducing innovative features that set it apart in the evolving browser landscape. Opera gained attention for its early focus on speed and customization, offering users a unique browsing experience.
Opera quickly became a choice for users seeking a fast and flexible alternative in the competitive browser market, leaving a lasting impact on the evolution of web browsing.

Microsoft began shipping Internet Explorer with their Windows operating system. Within 4 years, it had 75% of the market and by 1999 it had 99% of the market. The company faced antitrust litigation over the move, and Netscape decided to open source its codebase and created the not-for-profit Mozilla, which went on to create and release Firefox in 2002.

Realizing that having a browser monopoly wasn’t in the best interests of users and the open web, Firefox was created to provide choice for web users. By 2010, Mozilla Firefox and others had reduced Internet Explorer’s market share to 50%.

Other competitors emerged during the late ‘90s and early 2000s, including Opera(1996), Safari(2003), and Google Chrome(2008). Microsoft Edge replaced Internet Explorer with the release of Windows 10 in 2015.

Web Browsing Today and Future Opportunities

In the future, the web will likely stray further from its hypertext roots to become a vast sea of interactive experiences. Virtual reality has been on the horizon for decades, but the web may finally bring it to the masses.

Firefox now has support for WebVR and A-Frame, which let developers quickly and easily build virtual reality websites. Most modern mobile devices support WebVR, and can easily be used as headsets with simple cardboard cases. A 3D virtual reality web may be just around the corner. If that’s the case, the web browser itself may completely disappear and become a true window into another world.
